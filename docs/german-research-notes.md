# German Job Market: Research & Strategy

This document provides a practical overview of the German job market, specifically for international professionals aiming for a high-status career in tech or administration.

---

## 1. The C1 Proficiency Goal

Reaching the C1 level in German is a major professional milestone. It signals that you are an independent professional who can:
- Lead meetings and participate in technical discussions.
- Read legal and official documents without assistance.
- Manage professional correspondence with clients and authorities.

**Strategy**: Focus on "Linguistic Authority"—being precise, professional, and clear in every interaction.

---

## 2. Freelancing in Germany

Germany classifies self-employment into two main categories. Knowing which one you fall into is essential for tax planning.

### 2.1 Liberal Professions (Freiberufler)
- **Who**: Intellectual or artistic roles (e.g., Software Developers, Consultants, Teachers).
- **Benefits**: No trade license (*Gewerbeschein*) needed, simpler accounting, and exemption from trade tax below certain levels.
- **Requirement**: You must show that your specialized education directly qualifies you for the services you provide.

### 2.2 Commercial Trade (Gewerbe)
- **Who**: Retail, production, or any commercial activity not classified as "liberal."
- **Requirements**: You must register at the local Trade Office (*Gewerbeamt*).

## 4. Application Standards

### 4.1 The Resume (Lebenslauf)
- **Format**: Standard German resumes are **Tabular** and **Chronological**.
- **Detail**: Use clear, specific dates (Month/Year) for every role.
- **Professionalism**: A high-quality headshot is still a standard expectation in most German corporate environments.

### 4.2 The Cover Letter (Anschreiben)
Focus on the "Why." Explain clearly how your specific skills and background solve a problem for the company. Use a professional, direct tone.
